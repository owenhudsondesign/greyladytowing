Beach Driving Guide

How do you drive on the beach? There are a few things you should know to succeed.

Driving on Nantucket's beaches is an incredible experience—miles of pristine shoreline, stunning views, and access to spots you can't reach any other way. But beach driving requires preparation. Follow these guidelines to stay safe and avoid getting stuck.

Permits & Stickers

Before you drive on any Nantucket beach, you need the proper permits. Driving without a permit will result in fines—and more importantly, you won't be able to access the best spots.

Town of Nantucket Beach Sticker

This permit covers most Nantucket beaches including Surfside, Madaket, and many other popular areas. Available from the Town of Nantucket.

Trustees Great Point Sticker

Required for Great Point and Coatue—some of the island's most beautiful and remote beaches. This is a separate permit from the town sticker.

Pro Tip: Order Early

Order your permits before June for discounted pricing. The Gatehouse is the ideal location to purchase your Great Point permit—you can pick it up on your way out.

Vehicle Choice

Not every vehicle is suitable for beach driving. The right vehicle makes all the difference between a great day and an expensive rescue call.

Recommended Vehicles

  • Pickup trucks
  • Jeep Wranglers
  • Toyota 4Runners
  • Ford Broncos
  • Similar tall SUVs with genuine 4x4 systems

AWD vehicles can work but may struggle in soft sand. True 4WD with low range is ideal.

Not Recommended

  • Electric vehicles
  • Crossover SUVs with limited clearance
  • Sedans
  • Anything with two-wheel drive
  • Most rental cars

These vehicles lack the ground clearance, traction, or power needed for sand.

Tire Pressure

This is the single most important thing you can do to prevent getting stuck. Proper tire deflation is essential for beach driving.

Deflate to 15 PSI

For extremely soft sand, go down to 12 PSI. Lower pressure creates a larger tire footprint, helping you float on top of the sand rather than digging in.

Why It Works

When you lower your tire pressure, your tires spread out and create more surface area contact with the sand. Think of it like snowshoes—you're distributing your vehicle's weight over a larger area.

Don't Forget to Re-inflate

Once you're back on pavement, you'll need to re-inflate your tires before driving at normal speeds. Many gas stations on Nantucket have air compressors, or bring a portable inflator.

Using 4WD

Knowing when and how to use your 4WD system is crucial for beach driving success.

4HI (4-High)

Use 4HI for most beach driving conditions. This engages all four wheels while allowing you to maintain normal driving speeds on firmer sand.

4LO (4-Low)

Switch to 4LO when the sand gets particularly soft or when you need maximum torque at low speeds. This multiplies your engine's torque for more pulling power.

When to Switch to 4LO
  • Deep, soft sand
  • Climbing steep dune approaches
  • When your vehicle starts to struggle
  • After getting unstuck (to ensure you stay out)

Essential Driving Tips

Maintain Momentum

Keep your speed and momentum—this can get you over any obstacle or tricky spot. The worst thing you can do in sand is stop in a soft area. If you feel the vehicle slowing down, don't panic and accelerate hard. Instead, maintain steady throttle pressure.

Avoid Hard Braking or Acceleration

Sudden inputs dig you deeper into the sand. Smooth, gradual movements are key. If you need to stop, try to find firmer sand near the water line or in areas with tire tracks from other vehicles.

Follow Existing Tracks

Other vehicles have already packed down the sand. Driving in established tracks gives you firmer ground and reduces your chance of getting stuck.

Watch the Tides

Know when high tide is coming. Getting caught in soft sand as the water rises is dangerous. Check tide charts before you go and give yourself plenty of time to exit.

Respect the Environment

  • Never drive on dunes—it's illegal and damages fragile ecosystems
  • Stay on designated beach driving areas
  • Pack out all trash
  • Watch for nesting birds and wildlife

If You Get Stuck

Even experienced beach drivers get stuck sometimes. Here's what to do:

Don't Spin Your Wheels

This is the most common mistake. Spinning your tires just digs you deeper. As soon as you feel the vehicle stop moving forward, stop accelerating.

Try These Steps First

  • Check your tire pressure—you may need to deflate more
  • Switch to 4LO if you haven't already
  • Try rocking the vehicle gently between forward and reverse
  • Clear sand from around your tires
  • Place traction mats or boards under your tires if you have them

Know When to Call

If you've tried the basics and you're still stuck, don't make it worse. Call for help before you dig yourself in deeper or the tide comes in.
